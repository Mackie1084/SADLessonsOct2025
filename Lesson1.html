1.1 Introduction



Companies use information as a weapon in the battle to increase productivity, deliver quality products and services, maintain customer loyalty, and make sound decisions


Information systems are an integral part of organizations and are used to support operations, management, and decision-making

  • Business Processes

1.4.1 Business Processes

A business process is a set of related tasks or activities performed to achieve a specific organizational goal

1.4.2 Business Rules

Business rules are specific policies, procedures, or principles within an organization that guide its operations

1.4.3 Business Environment

The business environment encompasses all external and internal factors that affect a company's operations, strategies, and overall success

1.4.4 Organizational Culture

Organizational culture refers to the shared values, beliefs, norms, and practices that shape the behavior and interactions of individuals within an organization

1.5 How Business Uses Information Systems

  • In past, IT managers divided systems into categories based on the user group the system served
  • Today, it makes more sense to identify a system by its functions, rather than by users
  • Technology that improves productivity
  • Most large companies require systems that combine transaction processing, business support, knowledge management, and user productivity features

1.5.1 Enterprise Computing

Support company-wide operations and data management requirements

1.5.2 Transaction Processing

Efficient because they process a set of transaction-related commands as a group rather than individually

1.5.3 Business Support System

  • Provide job-related information to users at all levels of a company
  • Management information systems (MIS)
  • Radio frequency identification (RFID)

1.5.4 Knowledge Management Systems

  • Called expert systems
  • Simulate human reasoning by combining a knowledge base and inference rules
  • Many use fuzzy logic

1.7 System Development Tools and Techniques

Waterfall Model
Rapid Application Development (RAD)
Agile Development
Scrum
DevOps

1.7.1 Waterfall Model

A sequential design process, used in software development processes, in which progress is seen as flowing steadily downwards (like a waterfall) through several phases

1.7.2 Rapid Application Development (RAD)

An adaptive software development approach that emphasizes rapid prototyping and quick feedback over long drawn-out development and testing cycles

1.7.3 Agile Development

A group of software development methodologies based on iterative development, where requirements and solutions evolve through collaboration between self-organizing cross-functional teams

1.7.4 Scrum

An agile process framework for managing complex knowledge work, with an initial emphasis on software development, although it has been used in other fields including research, sales, marketing, and advanced technologies

1.7.5 DevOps

A set of practices that combines software development (Dev) and IT operations (Ops). It aims to shorten the systems development life cycle and provide continuous delivery with high software quality
